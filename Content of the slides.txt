SLIDE 6
Meet the Agentic Designer
Definition

An Agentic Designer is someone who designs how intelligent systems behave, collaborate, recover, and earn trust.

What they do
Define goals clearly
Architect context
Set constraints and permissions
Design workflows across humans and AI
Anticipate failure
Decide escalation paths
Balance automation with control
Example

A normal designer may design a travel booking UI.
An agentic designer designs:

what the assistant should ask
what data it needs
how it compares options
how it explains tradeoffs
when it needs approval before booking

Content

What really matters is:

context
memory

guardrails

Example

Weak prompt:
“Design a food delivery app for students.”

Stronger architecture:

User: sleep-deprived college student
Goal: get affordable food fast
Constraints: budget under ₹150, late-night options, hostel delivery
Agent behavior: recommend based on time, budget, and rating
UI tone: direct, fast, low-friction
Safety: confirm before payment
SLIDE 8
The 5 Layers of Agentic Design

This should be one of your strongest framework slides.

1. Intent

What is the actual goal?

2. Context

What does the system need to know?

3. Behavior

What should the system do step by step?

4. Guardrails

What should it never do without permission?

5. Feedback Loop

How does it learn, recover, or improve?

Example: Student Placement Assistant

Intent
Help a student prepare for placement interviews in 30 days

Context
Resume, target role, interview date, weak areas, company list

Behavior
Analyze resume, suggest improvements, create study plan, run mock questions

Guardrails
Do not fabricate achievements, do not auto-send applications

Feedback Loop
Adjust prep based on quiz performance and interview readiness

SLIDE 9
Designing Intelligence Means Designing Trust
Main statement

Questions to ask

SLIDE 10
Designing for Failure is Now a Core Skill
Content

In traditional design, failure meant broken flows.
In agentic systems, failure can mean:

wrong assumptions
overconfident actions
poor reasoning
misuse of tools
hallucinated answers
loss of user trust
Designers must define
fallback states
clarification patterns
confidence signals
escalation paths
recovery behavior
Example

Bad behavior:
AI books a meeting at the wrong time because it assumed availability.

Better behavior:
“I found two open slots tomorrow. Would you like me to schedule 11:30 AM or 4:00 PM?”

SLIDE 14
Prompt vs Context Architecture

This should be a powerful comparison slide.

Title

Same problem. Two very different design mindsets.

Example Problem

Build an AI study assistant for engineering students

Version 1: Prompt mindset

“Create a study assistant for students.”

Version 2: Architecture mindset
Users: 2nd and 3rd year engineering students
Goal: prepare for exams with less stress
Inputs: syllabus, calendar, current performance, available study hours
Behaviors: prioritize topics, schedule revision, detect backlog risk
Tone: encouraging, clear, non-judgmental
Guardrails: do not overload, do not create impossible study plans
Escalation: if student repeatedly misses tasks, recommend faculty or peer support
Closing line

One asks for a feature.
The other designs a system.

SLIDE 17
Human in the Loop vs Human on the Loop
Explain simply

Human in the loop
The human approves each important action

Human on the loop
The system acts, and the human supervises or intervenes when needed

Why it matters

Designers must choose the right control model depending on:

risk
speed
user confidence
consequence of failure
Example

Resume assistant: human on the loop
Payment assistant: human in the loop
Medical assistant: stronger human oversight

SLIDE 18
Mini Case: Self-Healing UI Components

Since your brief mentions self-healing UI, include it simply.

Title

What is a self-healing UI component?

Definition

A UI component that can detect issues, adapt to context, and recover gracefully without breaking the experience.

Example

A smart form field that:

notices invalid input
explains the issue clearly
auto-suggests correction
adapts keyboard/input mode
retries gracefully after failure
Better example

A login module that:

identifies whether failure is due to password, network, or session timeout
changes its response accordingly
offers the next best step instead of a generic error
Strong line

The best interface is not the one that never fails.
It is the one that fails intelligently.
